Class one | Aug. 29

Basic design principles

We'll cover four fundamental principles of design: proximity, alignment, contrast and repetition. This class pulls heavily from the classic text, The Non-Designer's Design Book, which I highly recommend (but don't require) you to read.

Resources:

  • The Non-Designer's Design Book (Preview, Ch. 1-4) – Robin Williams
  • Contrast And Similarity – Smashing Magazine

For next class

Redesign your resume using the four design principles we learned about in class.

Please also bring one example of good – or bad! – design with you to class next week and be prepared to talk about it. This can be anything, from a neighborhood flier to a magazine cover or piece of visual journalism.

Please read:

  • A 20 Minute Intro to Typography Basics
  • Fonts Have Feelings Too – Mikael Cho
  • An Introduction to Adobe Illustrator

Class two: Sep. 12

Design principles II

Typography plays a key role in good design. After an introduction to Illustrator, we'll cover the basics of typography and layout design. We'll also touch on color theory.

Resources:

  • Thinking With Type – Ellen Lupton

Please install Sublime Text before next class.

Please read:

  • Interactive Data Visualization for the Web: Ch. 3 – Scott Murray (please read up to the JavaScript section)
  • Responsive Web Design – A List Apart
  • Bitmap and Vector Graphics – BBC Bitesize

Class three: Sep. 19

Designing for the web

We'll review HTML/CSS and talk about best practices for designing for the web. We'll also open up our web inspectors to get a better look at how some of our favorite sites are structured and styled. Lastly, we'll take some time to talk about Photoshop and optimizing images for the web.

Resources:

  • The 30 CSS Selectors You Must Memorize
  • Meet Your Web Inspector – Dan Nguyen

Interaction design & usability

Course details

This class meets on Mondays, August 29 - October 6, from 6:00 - 8:50pm, except for CUNY-observed holidays.

Final project

The final project will be due on the last day of class, October 6 and will require you to design a web page for a piece of your own journalism.

This can be a data visualization or information graphic – or it can be a written article, designed according to the principles we've covered in class. You are welcome to redesign an existing project from another class, or you can report out a new piece.

All final projects will be submitted via Dropbox.

Grading

In addition to your final project, participation in class discussions and completion of exercises will be an important factor for your final grade.

The grading breakdown:

  • 50% Homework assignments & readings
  • 25% Class participation
  • 25% Final project

How letter grades will be assigned:

A: High quality student work with minimal editing required

B: Good quality work, requiring moderate editing and revisions

C: Unsatisfactory work, requiring heavy editing and revisions

F: Unacceptable, un-editable work
